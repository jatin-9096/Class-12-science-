1. Introduction to Electric Charges and Coulomb's Law
Status: Login to track your checkpoint metrics

Important Definitions

Electrostatics
The branch of physics focused on stationary electric charges, studying their interactions, forces, electric fields, and potentials in the complete absence of moving charges.
Electric Charge
A fundamental, intrinsic property of matter that causes an object to experience a physical force when placed within an electric field. It exists in two states: positive or negative.
Polarity of Charge
The property that distinguishes the two unique types of electric charges (positive and negative), which determines the specific direction of force and interaction between those charges.
Electrification
The physical process of an object acquiring an electric charge, most commonly achieved through friction, by directly transferring electrons from one material to another.
Gold-leaf Electroscope
A scientific device utilized to detect both the exact presence and the relative magnitude of an electric charge on a physical body by observing the divergence (spreading apart) of two extremely thin gold leaves.
Conductors
Materials that readily permit the flow and movement of electric charges across them. This is primarily due to the vast presence of free electrons capable of moving easily inside the material structure.
Insulators
Materials that strictly do not allow the free movement of electric charges through them easily, successfully maintaining a very high electrical resistance to any current flow.
Semiconductors
Materials exhibiting an electrical conductivity level that lies midway between conductors and insulators. Their specific conductivity is highly sensitive to external factors like temperature changes and added impurities (doping).
Point Charges
A hypothetical, idealized concept of charges assumed to be highly concentrated at a singular geometric point in space, used greatly to simplify the mathematical analysis of electric fields and forces.
Additivity of Charges
The fundamental principle stating that the total net electric charge of an entire system is simply the direct algebraic sum of all the individual point charges contained within that system.
Conservation of Charge
A universal principle indicating that the total net electric charge within an isolated system remains absolutely constant over time; indicating that electrical charges can neither be created out of nothing nor completely destroyed.
Quantisation of Charge
The physical principle that electric charge strictly exists in discrete, specific quantities. It is always found as an integral multiple of the basic elementary charge e (1.602 × 10-19 coulombs).
Coulomb's Law
The fundamental law stating that the electrostatic force of interaction between two stationary point charges is directly proportional to the product of their charge magnitudes and inversely proportional to the square of the distance separating them.
Permittivity of Free Space (ε0)
A fundamental physical constant (ε0) representing the inherent ability of a classical vacuum to permit the formation of electric fields, thereby influencing the strength of electric interactions. Its standard value is approximately 8.854 × 10-12 F/m.
Superposition Principle
The rule stating that the total net electrostatic force acting upon a specific charge is the exact mathematical vector sum of all the individual forces exerted on it by every other charge present in the vicinity.

Always Remember

  • The term 'Electricity' has its historical origins in the ancient Greek word 'elektron', which translates directly to amber.
  • Fundamental law of interaction: Like (similar) charges strongly repel one another, whereas unlike (opposite) charges attract each other.
  • The universally accepted historical naming convention of 'positive' and 'negative' charges was established by the scientist Benjamin Franklin.
  • Most physical objects exist in an electrically neutral state, which implies they possess an exactly equal number of positive and negative charges.
  • Both Coulomb's Law for electrostatic force and Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation strictly obey the principle of the Inverse Square Law.
  • The electrostatic force interacting between two charges is a vector quantity; thus, it can be mathematically expressed in vector form to accurately show both its magnitude and direction.

Important Formulae

1. Quantisation of Charge
$$q = ne$$
Where $n$ is any integer (positive or negative).
2. Coulomb's Law
$$F = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$
Where:
• $F$: Electrostatic force between two charges
• $k$: Coulomb's constant $\left(\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\right)$
• $q_1, q_2$: Point charges
• $r$: Distance between the charges
3. Coulomb's Force in Vector Form
For two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ located at position vectors $\mathbf{r}_1$ and $\mathbf{r}_2$: $$\vec{F}_{21} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r_{21}^2} \hat{r}_{21}$$
4. Forces Between Multiple Charges (Superposition)
$$\vec{F}_1 = \vec{F}_{12} + \vec{F}_{13} + ... + \vec{F}_{1n} = \frac{q_1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \sum_{i=2}^{n} \frac{q_i}{r_{1i}^2} \hat{r}_{1i}$$

Differences & Comparisons

Conductors vs. Insulators

Conductors Insulators
Allow free and rapid movement of electric charge. Strictly do not allow the free movement of electric charge.
Examples: Metals (Copper, Silver), human body. Examples: Rubber, glass, dry wood.
Can be easily charged using the method of induction. Primarily charged by the method of friction (rubbing).
Charges immediately distribute uniformly over the outer surface. Charges remain localized exactly where they were initially placed.
Generally good thermal conductors. Generally poor thermal conductors.

Positive vs. Negative Charge

Positive Charge Negative Charge
Fundamentally carried by protons. Fundamentally carried by electrons.
Represents a deficit (loss) of electrons in an atom or body. Represents an excess (gain) of electrons in an atom or body.
Attracts negative charges and repels other positive charges. Attracts positive charges and repels other negative charges.
Mathematically expressed as a positive (+) value. Mathematically expressed as a negative (-) value.
Determines the standard direction of conventional electric current. Flows exactly opposite to the direction of conventional current.

Electric Dipole vs. Point Charge

Electric Dipole Point Charge
Consists of two equal and opposite charges separated by a small distance. A single, isolated charge concentrated at one specific point.
Characterized by its dipole moment: $\vec{p} = q \cdot \vec{d}$ Characterized solely by its charge magnitude $q$.
Electric field strength decreases rapidly as $\frac{1}{r^3}$ at large distances. Electric field strength decreases relatively slower as $\frac{1}{r^2}$.
Has both magnitude and a specific spatial direction (Vector nature). Has only magnitude when considered as a scalar basic quantity.
Widely used to model molecular charge distributions (e.g., $H_2O$ molecules). The most fundamental and basic concept in electrostatics.

Real Life Application Based Questions

Q1. Why do we sometimes experience a shock when touching a metal door handle after walking on a carpet?
Answer: When you walk on a carpet, the friction between your shoes and the carpet causes a transfer of electrons. This builds up a static electric charge on your body (charging by friction). When you touch a good conducting object like a metal door handle, this built-up charge quickly discharges through the metal into the ground, resulting in the sudden sensation of a mild electric shock.
PART 2: Electric Field, Electric Field Lines and Electric Dipole

Important Definitions

Electric Field
A defined region of space around a charged particle where any other charged particle will experience an electrostatic force. It is mathematically and graphically represented by vectors.
Electric Field Lines
Imaginary, continuous lines used to visually represent the direction and strength of the electric field around a charge. They always point in the exact direction of the electric field and are drawn closer together in regions where the field is stronger.
Electric Flux
A measurable quantity representing the total number of electric field lines passing perpendicularly through a given surface area. It quantifies the flow of the electric field and heavily depends on the specific angle between the field lines and the surface.
Electric Dipole
An electric dipole simply consists of two exactly equal and opposite point charges ( $+q$ and $-q$ ) separated by a very small distance. It features a dipole moment that always points from the negative charge to the positive charge, characterizing its strength and orientation.
Dipole Moment
The dipole moment of an electric dipole is a fundamental vector quantity ($\vec{p}$) representing the strength and spatial orientation of the dipole. It is strictly defined as the product of the charge magnitude and the separation distance between the two charges.
Point Dipole
An idealized mathematical model used to accurately represent an electric dipole where the distance between the two constituent charges is infinitesimally small compared to the distance from the dipole to the observation point. It drastically simplifies calculations in dipole interactions.

Always Remember

  • The intensity of an electric field due to a single point charge decreases rapidly as the square of the distance ($\frac{1}{r^2}$).
  • The net electric field inside any solid or hollow conductor is strictly zero when in a state of electrostatic equilibrium.
  • Multiple electric fields can be successfully superposed (added using vector addition) to find the absolute net field at any given point.
  • The field of an electric dipole specifically refers to the unique, curved electric field pattern produced by the dipole in its surrounding 3D space.
  • When an electric dipole is carefully placed inside a uniform external electric field, it experiences a rotational torque that naturally tends to align the dipole moment parallel with the field direction.

Fundamental Characteristics of Field Lines

  • Two electric field lines strictly never cross or intersect one another at any point.
  • The field lines always emerge or terminate perpendicular to the surface of a charged conductor.
  • The absolute magnitude of the charge is directly proportional to the relative number of field lines originating or ending on it.
  • Geometrically, these lines always originate from a positive charge and successfully terminate at a negative charge.
  • If the system contains only a single isolated charge, the field lines will conceptually start or end at infinity.

Essential Mathematical Equations

1. Electric Field intensity due to a single Point Charge $Q$ at distance $r$
$$\vec{E}(\vec{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r^2} \hat{r}$$
Where:
• $\vec{E}$: Electric field vector
• $k$: Coulomb's constant $\left(\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\right)$
• $Q$: Source point charge
• $r$: Distance from the source charge
• $\hat{r} = \frac{\vec{r}}{r}$ is a unit vector pointing from the origin to point $r$.
2. Electric Field $\vec{E}$ due to a System of Multiple Charges
By applying the principle of superposition: $$\vec{E}(\vec{r}) = \vec{E}_1(\vec{r}) + \vec{E}_2(\vec{r}) + ... + \vec{E}_n(\vec{r})$$ $$\vec{E}(\vec{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{q_i}{r_{iP}^2} \hat{r}_{iP}$$
3. Electric Field of an Electric Dipole (At Large Distances, $r >> a$)
At a specific point on the Dipole Axis: $$\vec{E} = \frac{2\vec{p}}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^3}$$
At a specific point on the Equatorial Plane: $$\vec{E} = -\frac{\vec{p}}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^3}$$ *(Where $\vec{p}$ represents the dipole moment vector)*
4. Total Electric Flux ($\phi$) through a Surface $S$
$$\Delta\phi = \vec{E} \cdot \Delta\vec{S} = E \Delta S \cos\theta$$
The angle $\theta$ represents the exact angle formed between the Electric Field $\vec{E}$ and the area vector $\Delta\vec{S}$. For a complete surface: $$\phi \simeq \sum \vec{E} \cdot \Delta\vec{S}$$

Crucial Distinctions for Board Exams

Real Electric Field vs. Imaginary Field Lines

Electric Field Electric Field Lines
A fundamental vector quantity representing the electrostatic force experienced per unit test charge at a specific point in space. Imaginary visual lines drawn to perfectly represent the exact path a positive test charge would naturally take under the field's influence.
Strongly depends on both the magnitude of the source charge and the exact distance from it. Clearly indicate both the direction and the relative strength of the electric field in a region.
A real physical entity that can be accurately quantified and practically measured. They strictly do not exist physically, but serve as an exceptionally useful conceptual and graphical tool.
Clearly defined at every continuous point in 3D space. Conceptually start from positive charges and definitively end at negative charges.
Can be mathematically calculated using Coulomb's Law and the Superposition Principle. The physical density (closeness) of these lines visually indicates the field's absolute strength.
PART 3: Continuous Charge Distribution and Gauss's Law

Key Definitions & Concepts

Continuous Charge Distribution
A macroscopic situation where an electric charge is spread continuously and smoothly over a given region (line, surface, or volume), rather than being concentrated at distinct, discrete microscopic points.
Linear Charge Density ($\lambda$)
The defined amount of electric charge distributed per unit length of a charged 1D object, such as a wire. It is denoted by the symbol $\lambda$.
Surface Charge Density ($\sigma$)
The calculated amount of electric charge present per unit area on a 2D surface, commonly represented by the mathematical symbol $\sigma$.
Volume Charge Density ($\rho$)
A precise measure denoting the total amount of electric charge contained per unit volume within a specific 3D region of space, denoted by $\rho$.
Gauss's Law
A fundamental cornerstone principle of electromagnetism stating that the total net electric flux escaping through any closed surface is directly proportional to the total enclosed electric charge.
Surface Integral
A complex mathematical operation that involves integrating (summing up) a scalar or vector field uniformly over a defined physical surface.
Symmetry & Cylindrical Symmetry
Symmetry refers to the property of a system remaining unchanged under specific transformations (like rotations). Cylindrical Symmetry is a spatial symmetry where a system looks identical when rotated around a central cylindrical axis, greatly simplifying calculations for wires and cylinders.
Uniformly Charged Wire & Infinite Plane Sheet
A Uniformly Charged Wire has charge distributed evenly along its length (constant $\lambda$). An Infinite Plane Sheet is a theoretical 2D construct with a uniform surface charge density ($\sigma$) extending infinitely.

Important Facts for MCQs

  • The internal electric field everywhere inside a uniformly charged hollow spherical shell is always exactly zero.
  • For an infinitely long, straight, and uniformly charged wire, the electric field is directed radially outward (if positive) and steadily decreases with distance.
  • Gauss's law is exceptionally useful and time-saving for computing electric fields when the charge distribution possesses high geometric symmetry.
  • The electric field located just marginally outside the surface of a charged conductor is always perfectly perpendicular to that surface.
  • In a complete state of electrostatic equilibrium, all excess charges will naturally reside solely on the outer surface of a conductor.

Essential Formulae: Densities & Gauss's Law

1. Charge Densities
Surface Charge Density: $\sigma = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta S}$ (where $\Delta S$ is an area element)

Linear Charge Density: $\lambda = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta l}$ (where $\Delta l$ is a line element)

Volume Charge Density: $\rho = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta V}$ (where $\Delta V$ is a volume element)
2. Electric Flux through a closed surface $S$
$$\phi = \frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}$$ *(Where $q$ = total net charge enclosed by the surface $S$)*
3. Field Applications of Gauss's Law
Infinitely long straight uniformly charged wire: $$E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r} \hat{n}$$
Uniformly charged infinite plane sheet: $$E = \frac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0} \hat{n}$$
Uniformly charged thin spherical shell (Radius $R$):
• Field outside the shell ($r > R$): $E = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2} \hat{r}$
• Field inside the shell ($r < R$): $E = 0$

Key Distinctions for Exams

Continuous vs. Discrete Charge Distribution

Continuous Charge Distribution Discrete Charge Distribution
Charge is distributed smoothly and uniformly over a region. Charge is strictly localized at specific, individual points.
Described mathematically using charge density ($\lambda, \sigma, \rho$). Described mathematically using individual point charges ($q_1, q_2$).
Uses Integrals ($\int$) to calculate net electric fields. Uses Summation ($\sum$) to calculate net electric fields.
Highly applicable to larger, macroscopic physical scales. Highly applicable to microscopic, atomic, or molecular scales.

Gauss's Law vs. Coulomb's Law

Gauss's Law Coulomb's Law
Relates total electric flux through a closed surface directly to the charge enclosed. Describes the exact mechanical force acting between two isolated point charges.
Integral form: $\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}$ Formula: $F = k\frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$
Exceptionally useful for calculating fields in highly symmetric charge distributions. Universally applicable to any two specific point charges.
Forms a crucial part of Maxwell's fundamental equations of electromagnetism. Serves as the foundational experimental principle of classical electrostatics.
